two days. which is not enough. which is never enough. which is — and i've gone back over the itinerary three, maybe four times in the weeks since, turning it over like a stone that might have something underneath it — exactly forty-seven hours and change, Haneda to Haneda, if you count the cab at the end, which i do, because you have to count everything or what are you even doing.

---

5am. The city still dark when the wheels touched down. I remember thinking: *okay. okay. here we go.* I remember the vending machine on the platform at Haneda dispensing a coffee into my hands like it was the most natural transaction in the world (it was) (it wasn't) (both) and then the Yamanote Line pulling out of the station and the city beginning to unspool through the window — not dramatically, not like a reveal, just *there*, suddenly, the way Tokyo is just *there*, like it was always behind the glass waiting for you to look.

Shinjuku at 6am is a city that has just finished doing something it doesn't want to talk about. The streets have that specific quality of aftermath — not violence, not crime, just the residue of ten million private continuations. Someone slept here. Someone didn't sleep here. The convenience stores are all open and have been open all night and will remain open past the heat death of the universe, fluorescent and calm, selling onigiri to whoever comes.

I shot thirty-six frames before 8am and the light did something to them i still don't understand. Low, raking, February-specific — the kind of light that finds the edge of every surface and lights it up from underneath. Every vending machine was a lantern. Every puddle held a second city, inverted, slightly *wrong* — the kanji mirrored, the faces tilted, traffic lights reversed into something that looked like a message. I kept shooting the puddles. I shot so many puddles.

---

here is what i have been unable to explain to anyone who wasn't there:

the scale is wrong.

Not wrong like a mistake. Wrong like — you know that thing where a word stops looking like a word if you stare at it long enough? Tokyo does that to space. You'll be standing on a six-lane arterial watching a river of taxis and you'll step sideways through a gap between two buildings and suddenly you're in an alley barely wider than your shoulders, someone's laundry two feet above your head, a cat watching you from a doorstep with the expression of a thing that has long since made its peace with human strangeness. The city contains more cities. The cities contain more cities. I don't know how far down it goes. I didn't find the bottom.

Tsukiji, midday. The fish market proper left for Toyosu years ago but the outer market is still there, still going, vendors who've done the same eight square feet for thirty years. I ate sea urchin standing at a counter and something happened in my chest that i don't have a clean word for — gratitude, maybe, or the particular vertigo of being exactly where you are. I have tried to describe this to people since. *Good uni?* they say. *Yes,* i say. *Very good uni.* This is not what i mean. This is not even close to what i mean.

---

the old man in Ueno. i have to talk about the old man in Ueno.

He was on a bench. Bento box open on his lap. And he was *eating* — not performing eating, not eating the way you eat when you're aware of being seen, but eating with a quality of attention i have never observed on another human face, total and absolute, like the rice was a problem he had been working on his whole life and had finally, here, on this bench, in this particular February light, arrived at the solution. I raised the camera. I composed the frame. I had him — i had the light on the side of his face, i had the angle of his hands, i had the whole thing — and then he looked up.

He looked *directly into the lens.*

What i got instead was a photograph of a man looking at a camera. A man who *knew* he was being photographed. Which is a completely different kind of image, a completely different contract, a completely different thing. I kept it. I look at it sometimes now and think: that's still a picture of something. Just not the thing i was trying to take. Maybe the thing he gave me instead was more honest. Maybe he knew that. Maybe he was doing me a favour.

Or maybe i'm just making peace with the miss.

---

Second morning. Shimbashi. The *yokocho* — narrow lanes of shuttered izakayas, all of them closed until evening, the shutters painted or rusted or plastered with stickers advertising beers and ballgames and television shows from decades ago. I spent an hour photographing nothing but doors. This sounds like a joke but it wasn't. Every door was different. Every door was evidence. A padlock that looked sixty years old. A painted mural faded to almost nothing. A strip of tape over a crack in the metal, applied with enough care to suggest the crack had been distressing to someone. Doors are records. Doors are the part of a building that has been touched the most times by the most hands.

I didn't stay all day. The flight was at 7pm. I took the Keikyu Line back to Haneda in the early afternoon, the city unwinding in the window in reverse, and i looked at the frames on the back of the camera the way you look at something you're already not sure you actually saw — trying to verify. *Did that happen. Was that real. Did i get it.*

Some of it i got.

Some of it got away.

i'm going back. i keep saying this. i have not booked it yet. But i keep saying it, which feels like the beginning of something, or at least the middle.
